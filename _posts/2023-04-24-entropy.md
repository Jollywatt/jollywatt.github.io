The formula for information entropy

$$
H(ğ’‘) = -K \sum_{i} p_i \log_2 p_i
$$

is the **unique** real-valued function of finite probability distributions which:

1. is continuous with respect to $$ğ’‘$$,
2. obeys the _Composition Law_,
3. increases for uniform distributions $$ğ’‘ = (\frac1n, ..., \frac1n)$$ as the width $$n$$ increases.
4. has some fixed value $$K$$ for a fair coin toss $$ğ’‘ = (\frac12, \frac12)$$.

The last assumption is merely a choice of units â€” without it, the function is defined up to an overall scaling factor.

You can find strange and lofty explanations for why entropy is defined the way it is.
But this uniqueness property is my favourite characterisation, since the composition law (described below) is quite natural and pretty.

We will prove that any â€˜uncertaintyâ€™ function $$H(ğ’‘) â‰¡ H(p_1, ..., p_n)$$ satisfying

3. the property that $$H(\frac1n, â€¦, \frac1n)$$ increases with $$n$$

where $$K = H(\frac12, \frac12)$$.
The proof is in three steps, one for each property above:

1. Show that $$H(ğ’‘)$$ is uniquely defined for all distributions $$ğ’‘$$ if it is known for all _rational_ distributions $$ğ’’ âˆˆ â„š^n$$.
2. Show that $$H(ğ’’)$$ is uniquely defined for all rational distributions $$ğ’’$$ if the entropy of the uniform distribution

### Step 1.

This follows by the assumption of continuity. If $$ğ’‘ âˆˆ â„^n$$ is the limit a sequence of rational distributions $$ğ’’_i âˆˆ â„š^n$$, then by continuity $$H(ğ’‘) = \lim_i H(ğ’’_i)$$.
